Personal Development Journey

Reflections on "Twelve Pillars" by Jim Rohn & Chris Widener

About "Twelve Pillars"

"Twelve Pillars" by Jim Rohn and Chris Widener is a transformative guide presented as an engaging story about a young man named Michael who meets an extraordinary mentor, Mr. Davis. Through their conversations, Michael learns twelve fundamental principles for success and fulfillment in life.

The book masterfully combines narrative storytelling with practical wisdom, making profound concepts accessible and actionable. As I read Chapters 4-7, I found myself reflecting deeply on how these principles apply to my journey as a Computer Networking and Security student with a background in customer service, and how they can guide my professional development.

Below are my detailed reflections on each chapter, exploring not just what the authors teach, but how I can integrate these lessons into my daily life, career transition, and long-term aspirations in the technology field.

04

Chapter 4: Achieve Your Goals

Pages 43-52

Chapter Summary

Chapter 4 focuses on the importance of setting and achieving goals as a fundamental principle for success. Mr. Davis teaches Michael that goals provide direction, purpose, and motivation in life. Without clear goals, we drift aimlessly, reacting to circumstances rather than creating the life we desire. The chapter distinguishes between dreams (vague wishes) and goals (specific, actionable targets with deadlines).

The authors present a systematic approach to goal-setting: writing goals down, making them specific and measurable, setting deadlines, breaking them into smaller actionable steps, and reviewing them regularly. They emphasize the power of written goals and how they activate the subconscious mind to work toward achievement. The chapter also discusses overcoming obstacles, staying committed when challenges arise, and celebrating milestones along the way.

Key Takeaways

  • Goals give us direction and purpose in life
  • Written goals are significantly more likely to be achieved than mental goals
  • Goals should be specific, measurable, and time-bound
  • Break large goals into smaller, manageable steps
  • Review and adjust goals regularly to stay on track
  • Achieving goals builds confidence and momentum for bigger achievements

Personal Reflection & Application

Before reading this, I had vague aspirations like "work in IT" or "get certified," but these weren't real goals, they lacked specificity, deadlines, and action plans. After reading this chapter it has helped me clearly define my goals and the route to take in achieving them.

How This Applies to My Life:

From Dreams to Goals: This chapter helped me transform wishes into concrete goals. Instead of "I want to work in cybersecurity someday," my goal is now: "Secure a junior network administrator or IT security analyst position within six months of graduating in 2026, earning between $35,000-$45,000 annually, at a Jamaican company or international firm with Caribbean operations."

The specificity makes a huge difference. Now I can work backward from that goal to identify what I need: specific certifications (CompTIA Network+, Security+), hands-on experience (internships, labs), technical skills (firewall configuration, network monitoring), and soft skills (communication, problem-solving, customer service—which I already have).

Academic Goals with Deadlines: I've set clear academic milestones:

  • By End of Semester 2, AY 2025-2026: Pass all my courses with A- or higher
  • By End of 2025: Complete CompTIA Network+ study material and pass practice exams with 85%+ scores
  • By Mid 2026: Pass CompTIA Network+ certification exam
  • By May 2026: Complete BSc. in Computer Networking and Security

Breaking Down Big Goals: The chapter emphasizes breaking large goals into smaller steps. My goal of becoming a network security professional felt overwhelming until I broke it down:

  • Step 1: Excel in current coursework (daily study, complete assignments on time)
  • Step 2: Build foundational knowledge
  • Step 3: Gain certifications (Network+ by March 2026, Security+ by graduation)
  • Step 4: Get practical experience (apply for summer 2026 internships)
  • Step 5: Build portfolio (document projects, create GitHub presence)
  • Step 6: Network professionally (attend 2 tech events yearly, connect with 10 IT professionals)

Each step is manageable on its own, and completing each one builds momentum toward the ultimate goal.

This chapter taught me that achieving goals isn't about luck or talent, it's about clarity, commitment, and consistent action. My background in customer service gives me discipline and work ethic; now I'm channeling that energy toward specific, written goals that will transform my career aspirations into reality.

Chapter 5: The Proper Use of Time

Pages 53-62

Chapter Summary

In Chapter 5, Mr. Davis teaches Michael that time is our most valuable resource—once spent, it can never be recovered. The chapter emphasizes that successful people understand the value of time and use it wisely, while unsuccessful people waste time on activities that don't move them toward their goals. Time management isn't about doing more things; it's about doing the right things.

The authors discuss the importance of prioritization, eliminating time-wasters, and being intentional about how we spend each day. They introduce the concept that we all have the same 24 hours—what differentiates successful people is how they choose to use those hours. The chapter also addresses the need to say "no" to distractions and low-value activities in order to say "yes" to high-impact activities that align with our goals.

Key Takeaways

  • Saying "no" to distractions is saying "yes" to your goals
  • Plan your day/week in advance to use time intentionally
  • Eliminate or minimize time-wasters like excessive social media, TV, or unproductive activities
  • You can't save time, but you can invest it.

Personal Reflection & Application

This chapter hit me hard because I often feel like there aren't enough hours in the day, but this chapter showed me the issue isn't the amount of time; it's how I'm using it.

How This Applies to My Life:

Time Audit Reality Check: After reading this chapter, I conducted a brutally honest time audit for one week, tracking everything I did. The results were eye-opening. I was spending 2-3 hours daily on social media scrolling (Instagram, TikTok, WhatsApp conversations), 1-2 hours watching TV/streaming, and significant time on activities that didn't move me toward my goals. That's 15-20 hours per week—enough time to complete a certification study course or build several portfolio projects!

My goal is to spend 70% of my discretionary time on high-impact activities, 20% on necessary tasks, 10% on enjoyment, and eliminate time-wasters entirely.

Structured Daily Schedule: Instead of reacting to each day, I now plan my time intentionally:

  • 6:30 AM - 7:30 AM: Morning routine (exercise, breakfast, review daily goals)
  • 8:00 AM - 2:00 PM: Classes and lectures (when in session)
  • 2:30 PM - 5:30 PM: Focused study time (assignments, reading, lab work)
  • 5:30 PM - 7:00 PM: Dinner and family time
  • 7:00 PM - 9:00 PM: Certification study or portfolio projects (3-4 days per week)
  • 9:00 PM - 10:00 PM: Personal time (relaxation)
  • 10:30 PM: Bedtime to ensure 8 hours sleep

This chapter showed me that I do have enough time to achieve my goals; I just need to use it wisely. Every hour I invest in learning networking, practicing security configurations, or building my skills is an hour invested in my future career. Time is the great equalizer, I have the same 24 hours as successful cybersecurity professionals; the difference is in how I choose to use them.

Chapter 6: Surround Yourself With the Best People

Pages 63-71

Chapter Summary

Chapter 6 teaches that we become like the people we spend the most time with. Mr. Davis emphasizes to Michael that surrounding yourself with ambitious, positive, successful people elevates your thinking and behavior, while spending time with negative, unmotivated people pulls you down. The chapter introduces the concept that we are the average of the five people we spend the most time with.

The authors discuss the importance of being selective about relationships, seeking mentors who are where you want to be, and sometimes having difficult conversations with friends or family whose influence is holding you back. The chapter also emphasizes giving value to your network—being someone others want to associate with by bringing positivity, knowledge, and support to relationships.

Key Takeaways

  • You become like the five people you spend the most time with
  • Seek mentors and role models who embody the success you desire
  • Distance yourself from negative, toxic, or unmotivated people
  • Surround yourself with people who challenge you to grow
  • Give value to your relationships—be someone others want to associate with
  • Your network significantly impacts your opportunities and mindset

Personal Reflection & Application

This chapter was both inspiring and uncomfortable because it forced me to honestly evaluate my previous relationships and social circle. While I now have supportive family and friends, not everyone I interacted with were progressive and pushing me to do my best

How This Applies to My Life:

This chapter made me realize that spending too much time with people who don't value education or career development subtly influences my own motivation. When everyone around you is content with staying in the same place, it becomes easier to do the same.

Seeking Mentors and Role Models: I'm intentionally seeking relationships with people ahead of me professionally:

  • Senior Student: A final-year student completing an internship who provides advice on course selection, certification preparation, and job hunting strategies
  • Former Supervisor: My manager from Sangster's Book Stores who, while not in tech, demonstrates professionalism and work ethic I want to emulate

These relationships expose me to higher-level thinking and keep me motivated to push beyond my current limits.

Study Group Dynamics: I've formed a study group with two classmates who are equally serious about succeeding in Computer Networking and Security. We hold each other accountable, share resources, explain concepts to each other, and celebrate each other's achievements. This group has dramatically improved my understanding of complex topics because we push each other to truly master material rather than just memorize for exams.

This chapter taught me that my social environment is not something that just happens to me, it's something I can intentionally curate. By surrounding myself with people who are already where I want to be or are working toward similar goals, I naturally elevate my thinking, behavior, and expectations. My future success in cybersecurity will be significantly influenced by the network I build today.

Chapter 7: Be a Life-Long Learner

Pages 72-80

Chapter Summary

Chapter 7 emphasizes that formal education is just the beginning, true success requires a commitment to lifelong learning. Mr. Davis teaches Michael that the most successful people never stop learning, growing, and improving. They read books, attend seminars, seek new experiences, and constantly expand their knowledge and skills.

The authors argue that in today's rapidly changing world, those who stop learning become obsolete. The chapter discusses various forms of learning: reading, experiences, mentors, courses, and observations. It emphasizes that learning should be intentional and aligned with your goals. The chapter also addresses the importance of applying what you learn; knowledge without action is worthless.

Key Takeaways

  • Formal education provides foundation; self-education builds success
  • Successful people are voracious readers and continuous learners
  • Learn from multiple sources: books, experiences, mentors, courses

Personal Reflection & Application

This chapter speaks directly to my current situation as a Computer Networking and Security student. If there's any field where lifelong learning is absolutely mandatory, it's technology. Cybersecurity threats evolve constantly, new technologies emerge regularly, and yesterday's solutions become today's vulnerabilities. Stopping learning in this field means becoming obsolete quickly.

How This Applies to My Life:

Beyond the Classroom: While my BSc program provides essential foundational knowledge, I recognize that the university curriculum can't cover everything relevant to the fast-paced IT industry. This reality has pushed me into aggressive self-directed learning:

  • Studying for CompTIA certifications (Network+, Security+) outside of coursework
  • Watching YouTube channels that explain complex concepts practically
  • Practicing in virtual labs to gain hands-on experience

Learning From Experience: The chapter emphasizes experiential learning, and my work history has been an incredible teacher. My customer service roles taught me these lessons:

  • Problem-solving: Every difficult customer was a problem to solve—understanding their need, finding solutions, managing emotions. These skills apply directly to IT troubleshooting
  • Communication: Explaining products to diverse customers prepared me to explain technical concepts to non-technical users
  • Stress management: Handling busy seasons and demanding customers built resilience that helps when facing difficult coursework or complex technical challenges
  • Attention to detail: Inventory management and order processing required precision—one wrong number causes problems. Same with network configurations

Creating a Learning System: Inspired by this chapter, I've developed a structured learning approach:

  • Daily: 30 minutes reading technical content or security news
  • Weekly: 3-4 hours dedicated to certification study or skills practice
  • Monthly: Complete one technical book or online course
  • Quarterly: Learn one new tool or technology (Python scripting, Wireshark analysis, firewall configuration)
  • Annually: Earn one certification or demonstrate one major skill improvement

The Transformative Impact:

This chapter reinforced that my degree is a starting point, not a destination. When I graduate in 2026, I won't be "done learning", I'll be positioned to continue learning throughout my career. The technology field rewards those who stay current, adapt to change, and continuously expand their capabilities.
